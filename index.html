2. Accelerometer (ICM Series) - Motion Sensing

  • Physics to Electrical: MEMS accelerometers employ a "Comb-drive" capacitive architecture. The proof mass, suspended by micro-machined silicon springs, experiences inertial force F=ma when the device moves. This force displaces the mass, causing a change in the gap between interdigitated "comb fingers." These fingers act as the plates of a capacitor; as the gap (d) varies, the capacitance (C = εA/d) changes. The internal ASIC utilizes a switched-capacitor charge amplifier to convert these femto-farad scale capacitance changes into a linear voltage signal. For TDK InvenSense, managing the Spring Constant (k) is paramount to ensure sensitivity while maintaining structural integrity against shock. In professional TPM practice, we monitor Zero-g Offset and Sensitivity Error post-reflow, ensuring that thermal stress during SMT (Surface Mount Technology) does not permanently deform these micro-springs.
  • TDK Product Hub: TDK MEMS Accelerometers

3. Gyroscope (ICM Series) - Rotation Sensing

  • Physics to Electrical: Gyroscopes operate on the Coriolis Effect. A proof mass is electrostatically driven to oscillate at high frequency (the primary mode). When the sensor rotates, the Coriolis force (Fc = 2mv × Ω) acts perpendicularly to the velocity of the oscillation, inducing a secondary vibration mode (the sense mode). This secondary displacement is capacitively detected. The signal processing chain must decouple the high-frequency drive energy from the weak sense signal. TPMs must oversee the Q-Factor of the MEMS structure—higher Q-factors improve signal-to-noise ratios but require tighter vacuum packaging control. We manage this through rigorous cross-functional alignment between packaging engineers and ASIC designers to ensure thermal-mechanical stability over the entire operating temperature range.
  • TDK Product Hub: TDK MEMS Gyroscopes

4. MEMS Microphone (T58xx Series) - Acoustic Sensing

  • Physics to Electrical: A MEMS microphone features a thin, flexible diaphragm (often poly-silicon) suspended over a backplate. As sound waves strike the diaphragm, pressure differences induce mechanical displacement. This changes the capacitance between the diaphragm and the backplate. Because the capacitance change is minute, an internal charge pump provides a stable bias voltage to maintain the electric field. The ASIC then converts the capacitance fluctuation into a high-speed digital PDM (Pulse Density Modulation) stream. TPM oversight focuses on PSRR (Power Supply Rejection Ratio); if the power rails are noisy, the diaphragm's capacitive output will be corrupted, leading to audio artifacts. We enforce layout best practices to shield the PDM clock path from high-speed digital interferers in the system.
  • TDK Product Hub: TDK MEMS Microphones

5. Barometer (ICP Series) - Pressure Sensing

  • Physics to Electrical: Pressure sensors utilize the Piezo-resistive Effect. A micro-machined silicon diaphragm is exposed to ambient pressure. As pressure changes, the diaphragm deforms (bends). Integrated resistors on the diaphragm edges experience mechanical stress, which shifts their resistance values (ΔR). These resistors are configured in a Wheatstone Bridge circuit, which translates the resistance imbalance into a differential voltage signal. A critical TPM task is managing Post-reflow Drift. Since the diaphragm is sensitive to mechanical stress, PCB warping after soldering can "pre-load" the diaphragm, causing inaccurate pressure readings. We perform pre-sales RFQ assessments to ensure customers design proper "stress-relief" zones around the sensor footprint.
  • TDK Product Hub: TDK MEMS Pressure Sensors

II. Internal Physical Conversion Mechanisms of These Sensors

These MEMS sensors all utilize "micro-electromechanical" technology to convert external physical signals into electrical signals readable by ASIC circuits. The flow is as follows:

  • MEMS IMU (Accelerometer/Gyroscope):
    • Physical to Electrical Signal: Internally, there is a silicon "Proof Mass" supported by springs.
    • Mechanism: When the head turns, the proof mass displaces due to inertia, changing the "Capacitance gap" between it and the fixed comb fingers. By detecting this minute capacitance change, the circuit outputs digitized acceleration and angular velocity data.
  • TMR Position Sensor (Magnetoresistance):
    • Physical to Electrical Signal: Utilizes the "Tunnel Magnetoresistance" effect.
    • Mechanism: The sensor contains ferromagnetic layers and an insulating tunnel layer. When the external magnetic field (a magnet at the hinge of the glasses) changes angle, the resistance of the tunneling electrons flowing through the film changes significantly, thereby outputting a precise voltage value for the rotation angle.
  • MEMS Microphone:
    • Physical to Electrical Signal: A variable capacitor formed by a "Diaphragm" and a "Backplate."
    • Mechanism: Sound waves push the diaphragm to vibrate, changing the distance to the backplate, which modulates the capacitance value. The internal ASIC then converts this into a digital PCM audio format.
  • Ultrasonic ToF (Ultrasound):
    • Physical to Electrical Signal: "Piezoelectric Effect."
    • Mechanism: Emitting a voltage pulse causes the piezoelectric diaphragm to vibrate and emit ultrasound. When the sound waves reflect back, the pressure causes the diaphragm to undergo mechanical deformation again, converting this mechanical pressure back into a voltage signal via the piezoelectric material. By calculating the time difference from "emission to reception," the object's distance is accurately determined.

Article Analysis & Technological Comparison

This technical news highlights TDK InvenSense's architectural transition and superiority in MEMS pressure-sensing technologies, specifically contrasting traditional Piezoresistive methods with advanced Capacitive MEMS solutions for the rapidly growing low-power IoT (Internet of Things) market.

1. Piezoresistive MEMS Technology (The Traditional Approach)

  • Physical Mechanism: Piezoresistive sensors utilize a micro-machined silicon diaphragm implanted with strain-sensitive resistors (piezoresistors), usually arranged in a Wheatstone Bridge configuration. When ambient pressure changes, the diaphragm bends, causing mechanical stress that alters the resistance values.
  • Limitations in IoT: To detect these resistance changes, a continuous DC bias current must flow through the Wheatstone Bridge. This continuous current draw typically consumes tens to hundreds of microamps (µA), which severely limits the battery life of miniaturized IoT devices and wearables. Furthermore, piezoresistive elements are highly sensitive to temperature fluctuations, requiring complex calibration.

2. Capacitive MEMS Technology (TDK's Innovation)

  • Physical Mechanism: Capacitive pressure sensors consist of a flexible, conductive diaphragm and a fixed backplate separated by a microscopic gap, forming a variable capacitor (C = εA / d). Changes in atmospheric pressure cause the diaphragm to deflect, altering the gap distance (d) and thereby changing the capacitance.
  • Why it goes "Hand-in-Hand" with Low-Power IoT: Unlike resistive sensors, capacitive sensors do not require a constant DC current. They only consume power when charging and discharging the micro-capacitor to take a reading. This reduces the power consumption to ultra-low levels (often around 1 to 2 µA or less). Additionally, the capacitive architecture boasts lower intrinsic noise and greater temperature stability.
